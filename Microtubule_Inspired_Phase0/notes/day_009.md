## Temporal-resolution note

Current trajectories were saved every 1000 MD steps. With a timestep of 0.05 fs, consecutive saved frames are separated by 50 fs. Residence and persistence metrics are therefore resolved at 50-fs intervals.

## Initial field-free water–chromophore contact analysis

Water contact with the 24 fixed chromophore pseudo-atoms was evaluated using oxygen-to-nearest-site distances at 3.5, 4.5, and 6.0 Å.

A temporal-resolution correction was identified during analysis:

- Day 007 saved frames are separated by 2000 MD steps, corresponding to 100 fs.
- Day 008 replica01 saved frames are separated by 1000 MD steps, corresponding to 50 fs.

Contact counts and occupancy fractions are unaffected, but Day 007 residence-time estimates were recalculated using 100 fs between saved frames.

Main field-free observations:

- No water oxygens enter the 3.5 Å shell in either trajectory.
- The 4.5 Å shell is sparsely occupied, with fewer than one water oxygen per frame on average.
- The 6.0 Å shell contains approximately 13 water oxygens per frame:
  - Day 007 original: mean contact count = 13.875
  - Day 008 replica01: mean contact count = 12.905
- The number of waters ever entering the 6.0 Å shell is also similar:
  - Day 007 original: 30 waters
  - Day 008 replica01: 27 waters

Interpretation:

The 6.0 Å shell provides the most useful operational definition of the local chromophore-associated hydration environment. Its occupancy is reproducible between the original trajectory and the independent field-free replicate. The 4.5 Å shell represents rare close-contact events, while 3.5 Å is too restrictive for the carved geometry.

Residence-time values remain limited by trajectory output frequency and should be treated as discretized persistence estimates rather than fully resolved molecular residence times.

## Field-free versus fieldZ local hydration comparison

Water–chromophore contact statistics were compared between the original lead-hybrid trajectory and the independent replicate.

At the operational 6.0 Å cutoff, field-free local hydration occupancy was reproducible:

- original field-free trajectory: 13.875 waters per frame
- independent field-free replicate: 12.905 waters per frame

The response of this local hydration shell to fieldZ was not replicate-consistent:

- original trajectory:
  - fieldZ 0–10k: 16.636 waters per frame
  - fieldZ 10–20k: 19.182 waters per frame
- independent replicate:
  - fieldZ 0–10k: 12.455 waters per frame
  - fieldZ 10–20k: 10.909 waters per frame

Interpretation:

The approximately 6 Å chromophore-associated hydration environment is reproducible under field-free conditions. However, the direction and magnitude of its field-induced occupancy change remain trajectory-dependent at the present sampling level. Therefore, no general claim is yet made that fieldZ either increases or decreases local chromophore hydration.

This differs from the global axial water-dipole response, which was quantitatively reproducible between the original and replicate fieldZ trajectories. Global orientational ordering is therefore robust, while the local hydration rearrangement requires additional sampling and site-resolved analysis.

The 550 fs maximum residence values reported for the fieldZ segments equal the full 11-frame analysis window and are therefore right-censored persistence estimates rather than resolved exit times.

Next analysis:

1. merge each contiguous fieldZ trajectory across 0–20k, removing the duplicate 10k frame;
2. recalculate contact persistence over the complete field window;
3. resolve occupancy by individual pseudo-site or chromophore dipole pair;
4. identify whether different sites show opposing local responses that cancel in the global average.

## Early-versus-late fieldZ dipole-relaxation analysis

The fieldZ trajectories were separated into equivalent early and late windows:

- early fieldZ window: steps 0–5000, 250 fs
- late fieldZ window: steps 15000–20000, 250 fs

The raw orientational correlation increases substantially in the late window because of the persistent collective alignment generated by the field:

- early final C1 raw: approximately 0.726
- late final C1 raw: approximately 0.828

However, after subtracting the instantaneous collective dipole, the connected orientational relaxation is nearly unchanged.

Connected C1 at 0.25 ps:

- Day 007 early: 0.7591
- Day 007 late: 0.7609
- Day 008 replicate early: 0.7595
- Day 008 replicate late: 0.7579

Connected positive-window integrals over 0.25 ps:

- Day 007 early: 0.2111 ps
- Day 007 late: 0.2085 ps
- Day 008 replicate early: 0.2111 ps
- Day 008 replicate late: 0.2079 ps

Interpretation:

The applied field produces a strong and quantitatively reproducible collective axial polarization, but no detectable change in the short-time relaxation of orientational fluctuations after removal of the collective component. The apparent additional decorrelation observed when analyzing the complete 0–20k field trajectory arises primarily from the nonstationary polarization transient rather than from a change in the intrinsic fluctuation-relaxation dynamics.

Therefore, the robust field response at the present sampling level is collective orientational ordering. Local hydration occupancy remains site- and trajectory-dependent, while short-window connected dipole relaxation remains effectively invariant.

## Phase 1A transition: atomistic h-BN nanotube scaffold

During preparation for Phase 1, the structural validity of the Phase 0 BN-like scaffold was revisited.

Topology analysis confirmed that the Phase 0 scaffold is not an atomistic h-BN nanotube. Instead, it consists of a tubular arrangement of independent BN-like chains. Although this architecture successfully reproduced confined-water behavior and field-induced polarization effects, it is not chemically suitable for direct DFT/TDDFT calculations, realistic passivation, or excitonic parameterization.

A Phase 1A atomistic scaffold generation effort was therefore initiated.

### Initial generator attempt

The first h-BN nanotube builder produced an invalid geometry.

Observed problems:

- B-N distances approximately 0.725 Å
- large number of atomic overlaps
- unrealistic hydrogen placement
- incorrect local coordination

This structure was rejected.

### Corrected atomistic nanotube

The builder was rewritten using a rolled BN honeycomb lattice with a target B-N bond length of 1.45 Å.

Generated nanotube:

- circumference cells: 30
- axial rows: 28
- radius: 11.991 Å
- diameter: 23.983 Å
- B atoms: 840
- N atoms: 840

Structural audit:

- B-N bond count: 2460
- B-N minimum: 1.44950 Å
- B-N mean: 1.44967 Å
- B-N maximum: 1.45000 Å

The corrected geometry therefore reproduces the expected atomistic B-N bond length with negligible numerical variation.

### Passivated-edge test

A temporary edge-passivated version was constructed.

Results:

- all B and N atoms reached degree 3 coordination
- B-H and N-H bond lengths were physically reasonable
- however, 60 short H-H contacts appeared at the tube edges

Audit summary:

- overlap count = 60

Interpretation:

The passivation algorithm generates edge hydrogen collisions and therefore cannot yet be accepted as a chemically valid finite nanotube termination.

### Non-passivated scaffold audit

Hydrogen passivation was disabled and the nanotube regenerated.

Final audit:

- atoms total: 1680
- B atoms: 840
- N atoms: 840
- H atoms: 0
- B-N bonds: 2460
- overlap count: 0

No nonbonded contacts below 0.90 Å were detected.

Open edge atoms remain undercoordinated, as expected for a finite non-passivated nanotube.

### Current interpretation

The non-passivated nanotube is accepted as the first chemically interpretable atomistic scaffold of Phase 1A.

Current status:

- atomistic h-BN geometry: accepted
- structural audit: passed
- overlap audit: passed
- passivation strategy: not yet accepted
- force-field assignment: pending
- chromophore placement: pending

The scaffold is considered suitable as the structural baseline for subsequent chromophore integration and molecular-dynamics preparation, while edge-passivation methodology remains under development.
